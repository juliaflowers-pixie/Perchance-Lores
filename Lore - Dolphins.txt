# Overview:

 • A dolphin is any of 40 living species of aquatic mammal in the cetacean families Delphinidae (oceanic dolphins), Platanistidae, Iniidae, Pontoporiidae, and the probably extinct Lipotidae. All of these families belong to the order Odontoceti, i.e. toothed whales.

 • Dolphins range in size from the 1.7-meter-long human-sized dolphin to the 50-kilogram Maui dolphin to the 9.5-meter-long, 10-ton orcas.

 • They have streamlined, fish-like bodies, with the two front limbs evolved into flippers, complete loss of the hind limbs (with only a remnant of the pelvic skeleton), and a tail forming a horizontal fin.

 • Although not as flexible as semiaquatic seals, they are faster and more agile in the water; some dolphins can briefly move at speeds of 29 kilometers per hour or jump about 9 meters.

 • Dolphins use their conical teeth to hunt fast-moving prey such as forage fish, rattlesnakes and shrimp. They have a well-developed sense of hearing that is adapted to both air and water; so well-developed that some can survive even if they are blind.

 • Some species are well-adapted to diving to great depths. Like other aquatic mammals, they have a thick layer of subcutaneous adipose tissue, i.e. fat, which keeps the body warm in cold water.

 • Dolphins are widespread, mostly in pelagic marine environments, but some thrive in brackish and freshwater bodies. Most species prefer the warm waters of the tropics.

 • Like most nektonic animals, dolphins are carnivorous, feeding mainly on fish and squid, but a few large-bodied dolphins, such as killer whales, feed on larger marine animals.

 • Male dolphins typically mate with multiple females each year, but females only mate every two to three years. Pups are usually born in the spring and summer months, and the females take full responsibility for raising them. Mothers of some species fast and nurse their young for relatively long periods.

 • Dolphins are highly social animals, living in complex "fission and fusion" societies, and form variable groups (i.e. pods) that constantly change in size and composition.

 • Like other cetaceans, dolphins make a variety of vocalizations, usually in the form of high-pitched clicks and whistles, and are highly adept at echolocation.

 • Due to their gracefulness and high intelligence, dolphins play a prominent role in various cultures around the world, such as in art and folklore.

# Anatomy:

 • Dolphins have torpedo-shaped bodies with generally rigid necks, flipper-shaped limbs, a caudal fin, and bulbous heads. Dolphin skulls have small eye sockets, long snouts, and eyes located on the sides of the head. They lack external ear flaps.

 • Dolphins range in size from the 1.7 m long, 50 kg Maui dolphin to the 9.5 m long, 10 ton killer whale. Overall, they tend to be dwarfed compared to other cetarthiodactyls. Several species are sexually dimorphic, with females being larger than males.

 • Dolphins have conical teeth, unlike the spade-shaped teeth of porpoises. These conical teeth are used to hunt fast-moving prey such as fish, squid, or large mammals such as seals.

 • Breathing involves expelling stale air from the blowhole in an upward current that can be visible in cold air, and then inhaling fresh air into the lungs. Dolphins have relatively small, unidentifiable nasal tubes.

 • All dolphins have a thick layer of blubber, the thickness of which varies depending on the climate. This blubber can help with buoyancy, to some extent with protection, as predators would have difficulty penetrating a thick layer of blubber, and with energy for leaner seasons.

 • The blubber is primarily used for insulation from harsh climates. Young are usually born with a thin layer of blubber, which develops at different rates depending on the environment.

 • Dolphins have a two- or three-chambered stomach, which has a cellular structure similar to that of terrestrial carnivores. They have a fundal and pyloric chamber.

 • Dolphins' reproductive organs are located inside the body, with genital slits on the ventral (belly) side. Males have two slits, one covering the penis and one further back for the anus. Females have a single genital opening, which houses the vagina and anus, with a milky slit on either side.

# Intelligence:

 • Dolphins are known for learning, cooperation, scheming, and grieving. The neocortex of many species is home to elongated spindle neurons, which were known only in hominids before 2007. In humans, these cells are involved in social behavior, emotion, judgment, and theory of mind. Cetacean spindle neurons are located in brain regions that are analogous to those found in humans, suggesting that they serve a similar function.

 • Brain size was previously considered a major indicator of an animal's intelligence. Since most of the brain is used to maintain bodily functions, a larger ratio of brain mass to body mass may increase the amount of brain mass available for more complex cognitive tasks.

 • Some consider self-awareness to be a sign of high intelligence.The ability to think abstractly.

 • Self-awareness, although not scientifically well defined, is considered a precursor to more advanced processes such as metacognitive reasoning (thinking about thinking) that are typical of humans.

 • Research in this area suggests that cetaceans, among others, have self-awareness. The most widely used test of self-awareness in animals is the mirror test, in which an animal is presented with a mirror and then marked with a temporary dye. If the animal then approaches the mirror to examine the mark, strong evidence of self-awareness is demonstrated.

 • In 1995, Marten and Psarakos used television to test dolphins for self-awareness. They showed dolphins real-time video of themselves, video of another dolphin, and recorded footage. They concluded that their evidence was indicative of self-awareness rather than social behavior. Although this particular study has not been replicated since, dolphins have since passed the mirror test.

# Behavior:

 • Dolphins are highly social animals, often living in pods of up to twelve individuals, although pod size and structure vary greatly between species and locations. In areas with abundant food, pods may temporarily merge to form superpods.

 • Such groups can exceed 1,000 dolphins. Membership in pods is not strict; mutual exchange is common. They form strong social bonds and stay with injured or sick members, helping them breathe by bringing them to the surface if necessary.

 • This altruism does not appear to be limited to their own species. A Moko dolphin in New Zealand has been observed leading a female pygmy sperm whale and her calf out of shallow water where they have become stranded on several occasions. They have also been seen protecting swimmers from sharks by swimming in circles around them or lunging at them to scare them away.

 • Dolphins communicate using a variety of clicks, whistle-like sounds, and other vocalizations. Dolphins also use nonverbal communication through touch and posturing.

 • Dolphins also exhibit culture, something that was long thought to be unique to humans (and perhaps other primate species). In May 2005, an Indo-Pacific bottlenose dolphin (Tursiops aduncus) was discovered in Australia teaching its young to use tools.

 • They cover their snouts with sponges to protect them while foraging. This knowledge is usually passed down from mothers to daughters, unlike in primates where the knowledge is usually passed down to both sexes. Using sponges as mouth guards is a learned behavior.

 • Another learned behavior has been discovered in river dolphins in Brazil, where some male dolphins use weeds and sticks as part of their sexual display.

 • Forms of care between mates and even between members of different species have been recorded in various species—for example, trying to save weakened mates or female pilot whales holding dead calves for long periods of time.

 • Dolphins engage in aggressive acts towards each other. The older a male dolphin is, the more likely his body is to be covered in bite scars. Male dolphins may get into fights over mates and females. Acts of aggression can be so intense that the targeted dolphins sometimes go into exile after losing a fight.

# Sexuality:

 • Dolphins mate belly-to-belly; although many species engage in lengthy foreplay, the act itself is usually brief but may be repeated several times in a short period of time.

 • Gestation periods vary between species; for the small tucuxi dolphin, this period is approximately 11 to 12 months, while for the killer whale, the gestation period is approximately 17 months. Dolphins usually give birth to a single calf, which, unlike most other mammals, is usually born tail-first.

 • They usually become sexually active at a young age, before reaching sexual maturity. The age of sexual maturity varies by species and sex.

 • Dolphins are known to exhibit non-reproductive sexual behaviors, such as masturbation, stimulation of the genital area of ​​other individuals with the rostrum or flippers, and homosexual contact.

 • Various species of dolphins are known to engage in sexual behavior, including mating with dolphins of other species, and occasionally display sexual behavior toward other animals, including humans.

 • Sexual encounters can be violent, with male bottlenose dolphins sometimes displaying aggressive behavior toward both females and other males.

 • Male dolphins may also cooperate and attempt to herd females into heat, keeping them at bay using both physical aggression and intimidation to increase their chances of reproductive success.

Hybridization:
--------------

 • In 1933, three hybrid dolphins were found off the coast of Ireland; they were hybrids between bottlenose dolphins and bottlenose dolphins. This mating was later repeated in captivity, resulting in a hybrid calf.

 • In captivity, the hybrid offspring produced a bottlenose dolphin and a rough-toothed dolphin. A bottlenose dolphin-rough-toothed dolphin hybrid lives at SeaWorld in California. Other dolphin hybrids live in captivity around the world or have been reported in the wild, such as a bottlenose dolphin-Atlantic spotted dolphin hybrid.

 • The most famous hybrid is the wholphin, a hybrid of a false orca and a bottlenose dolphin. A wholphin is a fertile hybrid. Two wholphins currently live at Sea Life Park in Hawaii; the first was born in 1985 to a male false killer whale and a female bottlenose dolphin. Wholphins have also been observed in the wild.

Jumping and Playing:
-------------------

 • Dolphins often jump above the water surface for a variety of reasons. Jumping can save energy when traveling because there is less friction in the air. This type of travel is known as porpoise catching. Other reasons include orientation, social displays, fighting, nonverbal communication, entertainment, and attempting to release parasites.

 • Dolphins exhibit a variety of playful behaviors, often involving objects, homemade bubble rings, other dolphins, or other animals. When playing with objects or small animals, common behaviors include carrying the object or animal with various parts of the body, giving it to other members of the group or taking it from another member, or throwing it out of the water.

 • Dolphins have also been observed harassing animals in other ways, such as dragging birds underwater without showing any intention of eating them. Playful behaviors involving another species with the active participation of the other animal have also been observed.

 • The most obvious examples are dolphins' playful interactions with humans, followed by interactions with humpback whales and dogs.

 • Juvenile dolphins off the coast of Western Australia have been observed chasing, catching, and chewing on bloated fish. While some reports suggest that the dolphins are intoxicated by the tetrodotoxin in the fish's skin, other reports characterize this behavior as normal curiosity and exploration of the environment in which the dolphins are moving.

Tail-walking:
-------------

 • Although this behavior is very uncommon in wild dolphins, several Indo-Pacific bottlenose dolphins (Tursiops aduncus) from the Port River north of Adelaide, South Australia have been observed to “tail-walk.” This activity mimics a standing posture, using their tails to run backwards through the water. To perform this movement, the dolphin “protrudes most of its body vertically out of the water and maintains this position by vigorously flapping its tail.”

 • This began in 1988, when a female named Billie was rescued after becoming stranded in a polluted harbor and spent two weeks recovering with captive dolphins. Billie had previously been observed swimming and frolicking with racehorses exercising in the Port River in the 1980s.

 • After becoming stranded in a reedy estuary further offshore, she was rescued and placed with several captive dolphins at a marine park to recover. There, she observed captive dolphins performing tail walking. After being returned to the Port River, she continued to perform the trick, and another dolphin, Wave, imitated her. Wave, a very active tail walking dolphin, passed the skill on to her daughters, Ripple and Tallula.

 • After Billie's untimely death, Wave began tail walking much more frequently, and other dolphins in the group were observed performing the behavior as well. In 2011, up to 12 dolphins were observed tail walking, but only females appear to have learned the skill. In October 2021, a dolphin was observed tail walking for several hours.

 • The researchers found the spread of this behavior over two generations surprising, as it provides no apparent advantage and is very energy-intensive. A 2018 study by M. Bossley et al. suggested:
Social learning is the most likely mechanism for the introduction and spread of this unusual behavior, which has no known adaptive function. These observations demonstrate the potential power of spontaneous imitation in bottlenose dolphins and help explain the origin and spread of foraging specializations observed in multiple populations of this genus.

# Relationship with humans:

 • In Greek myths, dolphins have always been seen as helpers of humanity. Dolphins also seem to have been important to the Minoans, judging by artistic evidence from the ruins of the palace at Knossos. During excavations of the important Mycenaean city of Iklaina in 2009, a remarkable fragment of a wall painting depicting a boat with three human figures and dolphins was discovered.

 • Dolphins are common in Greek mythology, and many coins from ancient Greece have been found that depict a man, boy, or deity riding on the back of a dolphin. The ancient Greeks welcomed dolphins; seeing a dolphin riding in the wake of a ship was considered a good omen.

 • In ancient and later art, Cupid is often depicted riding a dolphin. The dolphin saved the poet Arion from drowning and brought him safely to land at Cape Matapan, a promontory forming the southernmost point of the Peloponnese. There stood a temple to Poseidon and a statue of Arion riding a dolphin.

 • Phalanthus was another legendary figure who, according to Pausanias, was safely brought to shore (in Italy) on the back of a dolphin.

 • Dionysus was once captured by Etruscan pirates, who mistook him for a wealthy prince for whom they could obtain a ransom. After the ship set sail, Dionysus invoked his divine power and caused a creeper to grow over the ship where the mast and sails had been.

 • He turned the oars into snakes, causing the sailors to be so frightened that they jumped overboard, but Dionysus took pity on them and transformed them into dolphins to spend their lives providing aid to those in need. Dolphins were also messengers of Poseidon and sometimes ran errands for him.

 • Dolphins were sacred to both Aphrodite and Apollo.

 • In Hindu mythology, the Ganges river dolphin is associated with Ganga, the deity of the Ganges River. It is said that the dolphin is among the creatures that heralded the goddess's descent from the heavens, and her pet, Makara, is sometimes depicted as a dolphin.

 • The Boto, a species of river dolphin that lives in the Amazon River, is considered to be shapeshifters, or encantados, capable of having children with human women.

 • Dolphins are most prominently represented on the Rapa Nui island of Rongorongo, and in Caroline Islands traditions they are depicted, similar to the Boto, as sexually active shapeshifters.
